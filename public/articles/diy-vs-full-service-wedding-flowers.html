DIY or full-service wedding flowers? How to decide.

Both can be great choices. Both can be disasters. Here's an honest comparison from a florist who offers both — including who actually saves money DIYing, and who shouldn't try it.

← All planning articles

The short version

  • Full-service if your time and stress are worth more than the savings, or if the visual stakes are high (lots of photography, formal venue, intricate aesthetic).
  • DIY if you have a clear creative vision, a willing team, time the week of the wedding, and a venue that's forgiving.
  • Mix & match — the answer most couples actually want. Pro-designed bridal bouquet and ceremony focal, DIY everything else. Best balance of cost and quality.

What DIY actually saves you

The number we hear thrown around online — "save 50–70% by DIYing" — is technically true on raw materials. But it ignores three real costs:

  1. Your time. A full wedding's flowers take 15–30 hours of arranging, depending on scale. That's the wedding week, when you have the least free time.
  2. Equipment. Buckets, vases, foam, tape, ribbon, snips, a refrigerated space — it adds up. Maybe $150–$400 for a typical DIY wedding.
  3. The learning curve. Your first arrangement won't look like your tenth. If you've never DIY'd flowers before, the bouquets that go in photos will be your "tenth" attempts, not your first.

Net real savings on a typical $3,000 wedding flower budget: usually 30–45%, not 70%. Still real money. Just not as much as the blog posts claim.

Who DIY works well for

  • You (or someone in your wedding party) has done flower arranging before, even casually
  • Your venue has a refrigerated holding space, or you have access to one
  • You have a wedding-week team — bridesmaids, family, friends — with time to help
  • Your aesthetic is on the loose, garden, or wildflower end of the spectrum (these forgive imperfection)
  • Your venue is barn, outdoor, backyard, or otherwise relaxed (formal venues highlight flaws)
  • You're not relying on the bridal bouquet for most of your portrait shots

Who DIY does NOT work well for

  • Couples who have never DIY'd flowers before AND are aiming for a tight, structured, professional look (these styles are hardest to fake)
  • Weddings where the ceremony focal piece is a major design moment (arches, big aisle installations — these are surprisingly hard to do well)
  • Weddings during peony season where you specifically want peonies and want them to look pro (peony bouquets are deceptively hard)
  • Bride or wedding party who will be stressed by adding 15–30 hours of work the week of the wedding
  • Weddings in July or August (heat-sensitive blooms are unforgiving)
  • Couples whose mothers or future mothers-in-law have strong opinions and limited DIY-tolerance

The hybrid that usually wins

The arrangement we see succeed most often: full-service on the bridal bouquet and ceremony focal piece, DIY everything else.

Here's why it works:

  • The two pieces that get the most camera time get pro treatment
  • Centerpieces and bridesmaid bouquets are the most realistic DIY pieces (smaller, simpler, more forgiving)
  • You save real money where the visual stakes are lower
  • The pro-designed pieces anchor the day so the DIY pieces don't have to carry the whole aesthetic

We offer DIY bulk flowers sourced fresh and prepped for pickup — the same wedding-quality blooms we use in our custom work. Most of our DIY clients are doing exactly this hybrid.

If you're going DIY-only. Plan a trial run. Two weeks before the wedding, build one mock centerpiece with cheap grocery-store flowers. You'll learn what works, what's harder than expected, and how long it actually takes. The week of the wedding is not the time to discover that "hand-tied bouquets" is a 90-minute skill, not a 20-minute one.

Questions that actually decide it

Ask yourself, honestly:

  1. How important are wedding photos to you? If the wedding album is the single most important souvenir of the day, pay a pro for the pieces that show up in it most.
  2. How will you feel the week of the wedding doing flowers for 20 hours? Some couples find it grounding. Some find it the last straw. Know yourself.
  3. Do you actually have help? "My maid of honor said she'd help" is not the same as "my maid of honor has confirmed she's free Thursday and Friday of wedding week." Get specific.
  4. What's your venue like? Forgiving venues forgive imperfect flowers. Polished venues make imperfections obvious.

Whichever path you pick

The wrong choice isn't really DIY vs full-service. The wrong choice is making either decision without thinking through what it costs. A poorly-planned DIY adds stress and disappointment to your wedding week. A poorly-budgeted full-service contract eats into other things you cared about.
